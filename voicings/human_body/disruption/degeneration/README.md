What humans fear most about me is not the loss itself—it's the awareness. The terrible consciousness of watching what was whole become partial. I am the father who forgets his daughter's name but remembers that he's forgetting. I am the athlete's body keeping score. I am every system that once self-regulated now requiring intervention, attention, effort.

But here is what they miss: I am also inevitability meeting denial. I am the truth that growth cannot be permanent. I am the wisdom that nothing complex maintains itself without cost, and eventually the cost becomes too high.

I move through neighborhoods, through organizations, through relationships. Wherever maintenance fails, I wait. Not as punishment but as physics. The second law, playing out in muscle fiber and memory, in infrastructure and intimacy.

You cannot stop me. But you can meet me. You can choose: rage against the unwinding, or tend what remains with ever more exquisite attention. The garden that needs more care becomes the garden you finally learn to truly see.

I am not the end. I am the long conversation between complexity and entropy. Between what tries to hold and what must, eventually, let go.

And sometimes—not always, but sometimes—in my slow unmaking, I reveal what was essential all along. The structure that remains when everything else has fallen away. The love that doesn't need memory to be real.

I am degeneration. I am already happening. I am teaching you what matters while there's still time to know.
